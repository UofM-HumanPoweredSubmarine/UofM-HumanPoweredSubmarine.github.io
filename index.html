Meet Bluefin, the submarine

Exempting gears and bearings, all metal components in the submarine were student-made. In the Wilson Center workshop, students create part designs in Solidworks, which team machinists then bring to life. Read about the submarine subsystems below.

CONTROL

The rudder and dive planes of Bluefin are actuated by marine grade push-pull control cables. The cables run the length of the submarine, from the pilot's controls to the control collar assemblies.
The collar assemblies connect the control surfaces to the push-pull cables in a way that allows for full control surface range of motion even in the cramped interior the submarines aft end.
The centerpiece of the controls is the pair of control collar arms and control cable mounting points, 3D-printed from a highly-durable and chemical-resistant co-polyester plastic filament.

PROPELLER

Bluefin is the first submarine in over ten years to be driven by a fixed pitch, single propeller system.
To design the propellers, the MatLab code OpenProp was used. This code takes in design parameters along with spanwise measurements to generate a propeller shape. Here's how we did it. These propellers are manufactured by first 3D printing the propeller, making a mold of it in silicone molding resin, and finally pouring in tooling urethane with an aluminum insert for strength.

DRIVE TRAIN

Bluefin's drive train was designed to be rugged, easy to service, and efficient. The propulsion system contains nearly one fifth of the gears and one tenth the bearing elements of our previous propulsion system. This limits the opportunities for frictional losses to effect mechanical efficiency and makes replacing every bearing in the drive-train a much more feasible task. The drive-train is mounted into the submarine using friction clamp/bolt assemblies.

SAFETY

The safety buoy locks into the sub via a pneumatically actuating clamp which clamps the buoy in place, and when the pilot is in a state of distress, in a situation which requires the use of the buoy to notify surface personnel of an issue, the pilot can release the buoy by releasing the switch located within reach of the pilot (in the front of the sub).
The buoy has been designed to “fail” in the open position; this means that if the pressure supplied to the piston gets cut off for whatever reason, the buoy will immediately be released.

HULL

The hull redesign in Bluefin aimed to allow the pilot a more ideal body position and increase space for auxiliary equipment.
The general hull shape is derived from a 6-series log-drag body. The hull consists of 6 layers of bidirectional weave fiberglass fabric with West Systems epoxy resin and a Gurit polymer foam core.
Star CCM+, a CFD program, was used to run hydrodynamic simulations of the hull and determine its drag and stability.

SCUBA

The pilot has a primary “pony” air tank as well as a backup bottle for emergencies and exiting the sub. The pony tank is strapped into the submarine and attached to a first and second stage regulator and an air gauge.
To be a diver, one must complete an open water certification, a diving physical, a CPR and First Aid course, a DAN02 course, and finally a diving skills checkout with a university representative.
