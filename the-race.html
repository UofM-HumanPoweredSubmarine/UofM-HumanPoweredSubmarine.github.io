Submarine Races

The annual design competition sponsored by Foundation for Underwater Research & Education (FURE) for human powered underwater vehicles provides valuable educational experiences to the best and brightest engineering and science students from throughout the world.

The race venue alternates between the International Submarine Race TM (ISR) at the Naval Surface Warfare Center, Carderock Division (NSWC), in Bethesda, Maryland, USA and the European International Submarine Race (eISR) at QinetiQ Ocean Basin in Gosport, UK.

Submarines compete on more than finishing speed. Judges rate teams’ design report and submarine reliability to consider a submarine holistically for engineering innovation, material design, and team performance.

(Award descriptions are taken directly from the official ISR website).

Absolute Speed Award

Top speed (kts) submarine scross all teams and all categories.

Fastest by Category

1 or 2 man boat, propeller or non-propeller, Independent, High School or College level.

Innovation Award

Most innovative design, construction and or performance attribute.


Best Use of Composites

Best use of composites in the boat, such as recycled carbon fiber.

Smooth Operator

Efficiency in staging for the course, racing the course, and troubleshooting.

Best Design Outline

Best design outline report explaining construction submitted on their submarine.


Overall Performance

Highest Figure of Merit (FOM), determined by 17 weighted parameters.

Best Spirit of the Race

Best gusto, fortitude, support for the other teams, selected by the teams themselves.

ISR Fun Facts

During the American Revolutionary War, a continental army human-powered submarine Turtle tried and failed to sink the British warship HMS Eagle.

The pioneer of human-powered submarine racing Henry A. “Hap” Perry also built the vehicle James Bond 007 used in Thunderball.

The first 3 ISRs were hosted in open waters, off the Florida coasts.

212 teams and 87 submarines have entered the races historically.

The world speed record for a human-powered submarine is just over 8 knots (9.2 mph), achieved by École de technologie supérieure of Montreal's Omer 5.

The Navy uses the massive Bethesda race pool to drag warships and warship parts through the water in hydrodynamic tests.
